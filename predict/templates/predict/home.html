Heart disease is also called cardiovascular disease, which continuously rises every day. In the 2016 WHO survey, an estimated 17 million people die yearly from cardiovascular diseases (CVDs) worldwide, more so from heart and stroke. The prevalent symptoms and health behaviors that lead at an early stage to the development of cardiovascular diseases must be established. However, different studies are performed to assess cardiovascular diseases. Blood pressure, blood sugar, cholesterol etc. are some of these measures.

A category of diseases covering the blood vessels (arteries and veins) and the heart are cardiovascular diseases. There is a high and troubling rate of cardiovascular disease in many countries around the world at present. According to WHO, among men and women over sixty-five years of age, cardiovascular disorders have been a principal source of debilitation and death worldwide. Cardiovascular disease is presently seen as a "second epidemic," in many nations, replacing infectious diseases as the leading cause of death. Cardiovascular disorders were initially viewed as a health concern intended for developing countries. It is still a significant health problem today for developed countries as well. In reality, for developing countries, it is more surprising than inadequate health care. Cardiovascular disease has been believed to be a disease reserved for men in the recent past, but lately it is also considered to be one of women's killer diseases.

By diagnosing cardiovascular disease by medical assessment, history and physical examination, and early diagnosis of heart disease, the mortality rate may also be reduced. One of the finest approaches to cardiovascular disease diagnosis is echocardiography. A painless echocardiography test, or echo test. It uses sound waves to create representations of the heart. The echocardiography test collects data relating to the heart's shape and scale. It also dictates how well the heart and valve chambers function, too. This test is also used for the early identification of children and infants with cardiovascular disease. Echocardiography is often used to identify areas that do not usually contract in the muscle of the heart due to inadequate blood flow or heart attack injury.
